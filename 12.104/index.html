resources

online references

The following links point to online material to which I frequently refer.

  • Periodic Table of Elements in the Ocean (MBARI) - This is an excellent starting point for getting order-of-magnitude estimates about the residence time, speciation, quantitation methods, and general behavior for all naturally-occuring elements in the modern ocean.

book recommendations

  • Quantitative Chemical Analysis, 6e - Harris - This is probably the best quantitative analysis book on the market, and is written in a conversational, easy-to-follow, style. Do yourself a favor and pick up an inexpensive older edition (6th or 7th is fine). Describes in depth, but doesn't belabor discussion of, both 'old-school' (e.g., titrations, gravimetry, voltammetry) and modern techniques and instruments (e.g., high-resolution mass spectrometry) commonly used in geochemistry and other branches of science that draw heavily on analytical chemistry. The tables in the back alone justify the cost.
  • Tracers in the Sea - Broecker & Peng - The classic text on chemical oceanography. Out-of-print, but now available freely online (see the link) as a PDF.

Image of the Week #1

Crystal Geyser, Green River, Utah

A CO2-rich cold spring demonstrating the precipitation of calcium carbonate upon exposure to air. The striking orange color is due to ferric iron (essentially rust) incorporated into the travertine terraces.

Image of the Week #2

Spiral Jetty, Great Salt Lake, Utah

The North Arm of the Great Salt Lake has a salinity ~10 times that of seawater. Ooids (rounded mm-sized grains of calcium carbonate) precipitate from the waters. When working on the thermodynamics in this high-ionic strength system, it's essential to consider non-ideal corrections to the activities of dissolved species. (photo by A. Close)
